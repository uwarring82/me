About

I am an experimental physicist at the University of Freiburg, working in the Quantum & Atomic Physics group led by Tobias Schätz. My work centres on the control of trapped atomic ions — using them as a platform for quantum simulation, precision measurement, and the study of fundamental processes in open quantum systems.

I received my PhD from the Ruprecht-Karls-Universität Heidelberg in 2009, supervised by Alban Kellerbauer at the Max-Planck-Institut für Kernphysik. There I worked on high-resolution laser spectroscopy of the osmium anion, contributing to the first optical hyperfine structure measurement in an atomic anion. The precision techniques and careful systematic reasoning I learned in Heidelberg have shaped my experimental approach ever since.

From there I moved to the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) in Boulder, Colorado, where I joined the ion-storage group and worked with Dietrich Leibfried and colleagues on microwave-based quantum control of trapped ions. That period was formative: it introduced me to the trapped-ion toolbox that has defined my research in Freiburg, where I have been a research associate since 2013.

Teaching and mentoring have become increasingly important to me over the years. I coordinate advanced laboratory courses and seminars, and I value the exchange of ideas with students and colleagues as an essential part of scientific work.

I also maintain the Open-Science Harbour, a documentation framework for trapped-ion research that separates invariant physical constraints from local experimental practices — an attempt to organise knowledge without claiming final authority over truth.

Research

My research focuses on the control of trapped atomic ions as a platform for studying quantum dynamics, quantum thermodynamics, and precision measurement. Across these areas, a common theme is the use of highly controlled few-body systems to probe fundamental questions about quantum behaviour while simultaneously developing tools relevant for quantum technologies. Active research areas include:

  • Open quantum system dynamics in the ultrastrong coupling regime
  • Two-dimensional ion trap architectures for quantum simulation
  • Experimental studies of relaxation ordering and quantum Mpemba effects
  • Precision spectroscopy and ion-based sensing techniques

Quantum Control and Simulation with Trapped Ions

Our group develops trapped-ion architectures for multi-dimensional quantum simulations. By trapping ions in reconfigurable two-dimensional arrays with individual control, we can implement quantum simulation protocols beyond what is possible in linear ion strings. This work builds on microwave-based quantum logic techniques developed during my time at NIST, where we demonstrated that microwave field gradients can address individual ions — opening a path toward scalable quantum architectures without the complexity of tightly focused laser beams.

Open Quantum Systems and Quantum Thermodynamics

A central theme of our current work is understanding how strong coupling and memory effects shape the dynamics of open quantum systems. Using a single trapped magnesium ion as a precisely controlled two-level system coupled to an engineered bosonic environment, we investigate phenomena that challenge standard weak-coupling descriptions.

Our most recent result demonstrates time-dependent energy level renormalisation in the ultrastrong coupling regime — energy shifts of up to 15% of the bare system frequency, arising purely from system–environment correlations. This work, carried out with Alessandra Colla and Heinz-Peter Breuer, provides experimental support for recent theoretical approaches to minimal-dissipation dynamics in quantum thermodynamics.

I also authored an APS Physics Viewpoint reviewing three studies on quantum versions of the Mpemba effect — the surprising phenomenon in which a hotter system can relax to equilibrium faster than a cooler one.

  • Clos, Porras, Warring, Schaetz, “Time-resolved observation of thermalization in an isolated quantum system,” Phys. Rev. Lett. 117, 170401 (2016). doi:10.1103/PhysRevLett.117.170401

Analogue Quantum Phenomena

We have used trapped-ion systems to explore phenomena typically associated with relativistic quantum field theory in a controlled laboratory setting. By parametrically driving a trapped ion, we demonstrated phonon pair creation from inflated quantum fluctuations — a table-top analogue of the dynamical Casimir effect.

Selected publication

  • Wittemer, Hakelberg, Kiefer, Schröder, Fey, Schützhold, Warring, Schaetz, “Phonon pair creation by inflating quantum fluctuations in an ion trap,” Phys. Rev. Lett. 123, 180502 (2019). doi:10.1103/PhysRevLett.123.180502

Open Science

I maintain the Open-Science Harbour, a documentation framework for trapped-ion quantum physics research at the University of Freiburg.

The Harbour separates invariant constraints (physical limits, mathematical structures) from local experimental practices (equipment choices, procedures). Documents are labelled by scope — Global (G) or Local (L) — and by role: coastlines define boundaries, essays provide interpretive frameworks, and handbooks record operational practice.

This structure reflects a commitment to transparency, reproducibility, and epistemic honesty: we work with models, frameworks, and measurements, and we do not know with certainty how close we come to true values — only whether our methods are internally consistent and externally testable.
