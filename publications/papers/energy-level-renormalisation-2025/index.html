Observing time-dependent energy level renormalisation in an ultrastrongly coupled open system

A. Colla, J. Hasse, K. R. Palani, T. Schaetz, H.-P. Breuer, U. WarringORCID

Nature Communications 16, 2502 (2025) · Published 12 March 2025 · DOI: 10.1038/s41467-025-57840-4

Read at publisher → arXiv: 2408.15928

Author summary

How do the energy levels of a quantum system shift when it is strongly coupled to its environment? Standard open-system treatments assume the system’s intrinsic spectrum is unchanged and that only dissipation broadens or shifts it. In the ultrastrong coupling regime this assumption breaks. Using a single trapped magnesium ion coupled to an engineered bosonic environment, we measured time-dependent renormalisation of the system’s energy levels during the approach to steady state — shifts reaching 15% of the bare system frequency, driven purely by system–environment correlations. The result supports recent theoretical approaches that treat environment-induced level shifts and dissipation as co-equal ingredients rather than separate corrections, and it establishes a platform where minimal-dissipation dynamics relevant to quantum thermodynamics can be tested experimentally. The measurement combines the trapped-ion toolbox for individual control with bath engineering that would be difficult to replicate in solid-state analogues.

Why this mattered

In the weakly coupled regime, system and bath are conceptually separable; in the ultrastrong regime they are not. Making that non-separability visible in a controlled system — and measuring it in real time — gives theory a concrete object to test against and gives quantum thermodynamics a laboratory handle on what “the system” even means when it is entangled with its environment. Collaboration with A. Colla and H.-P. Breuer connected an experimental result to the minimal-dissipation line of theoretical work on open systems.
