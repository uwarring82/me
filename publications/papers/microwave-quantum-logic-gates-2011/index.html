Microwave quantum logic gates for trapped ions

C. Ospelkaus, U. WarringORCID, Y. Colombe, K. R. Brown, J. M. Amini, D. Leibfried, D. J. Wineland

Nature 476, 181–184 (2011) · Published 11 August 2011 · DOI: 10.1038/nature10290

Read at publisher →

Author summary

Can scalable trapped-ion quantum logic be built without tightly focused laser beams? Laser-based gates on trapped ions are experimentally powerful but become harder to scale as the number of ions grows: each gate requires a beam delivered to a specific ion with high fidelity, and cross-talk, phase stability, and wavefront errors all compound. We demonstrated at the National Institute of Standards and Technology that engineered microwave near-field gradients — produced by currents flowing in electrodes of the ion trap itself — can drive single-qubit rotations and two-qubit entangling gates on individually addressed ions. Microwaves are mature, stable, and routed by geometry rather than by optics. The proof-of-principle Bell-state fidelity matched contemporary laser-based approaches. The paper opened a line of trapped-ion architecture development in which the control hardware scales with the trap chip rather than with beam delivery, and it underwrites the microwave-based control lines in our current Freiburg 2D-array work.

Why this mattered

At the time, the field largely assumed that laser-driven Raman gates were the only path forward for ion-trap quantum computing. Showing that microwave near-fields could deliver equivalent fidelity — using hardware that scales naturally with ion-trap fabrication — opened a second architecture for the community and for our own subsequent programme on reconfigurable 2D arrays.
